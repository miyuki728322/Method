Orientation

In a Japanese meal, rice is not the side. It is the centre — the axis every other dish is seasoned to support. The word ご飯 means both cooked rice and the meal itself. Cook the rice well and the table is already most of the way there.

Four turns that shaped the grain

Domains are measured in koku of rice. One koku ≈ a person's rice for a year. Rice is tax, wealth, and a samurai's stipend.

The Difference

Why Japanese rice stands apart

01 — Starch
Starch compositionアミロペクチン
Short-grain japonica runs high in amylopectin and low in amylose (~17%). Amylopectin gives the cling, the gloss, and the gentle chew — the mochi-mochi texture. Premium cultivars push amylose lower still.
02 — Breeding
Precision breeding品種改良
Decades of public breeding stations select for sweetness, cold tolerance, aroma, and grain integrity. Named cultivars are tuned as deliberately as wine grapes — each with its own character.
03 — Terroir
Terroir & water風土と水
Snowmelt water, mineral soils, and wide day-to-night temperature swings — the conditions of Niigata or Hokkaido — concentrate sugar and aroma in the grain as it ripens slowly.

The Method

Treat rice as a technique, not a default

Five stages, each with its own discipline. Skip none of them. The whole sequence runs about an hour, most of it unattended — but the order and the resting are not negotiable.

01
Wash研ぐ
Rinse off surface starch dust until the water runs translucent.
02
Hydrate浸水
Rest the drained grain 30 min so it absorbs water evenly to the core.
03
Measure計量
Weigh rice and water to a ratio — never measure by volume or eye.
04
Cook炊く
Follow the heat curve to boil, then low simmer, then off the heat.
05
Rest蒸らす
Steam 10 minutes lid-on, then fluff to release moisture and clumping.

The Method · Wash

Why we wash

01
Remove surface starch dust
Milling and transport shed loose starch onto the grain. Cook it in and you get a gummy, cloudy, dull pot instead of distinct, glossy grains.
02
Clear storage off-aromas
A first rinse carries away the faint stale note rice picks up in the bag, so the clean, sweet aroma of the grain itself comes through.
03
Begin even hydration
Washing is the first contact with water. The grain starts drinking immediately — which is exactly why the first water matters most.
04
Modern rice is not old rice
Today's rice carries no talc or heavy bran coat. The hard scrubbing of the past is obsolete — we rinse, we don't polish.
The goal: translucent water and intact grains — not crystal-clear water won by crushing the rice.

The Method · Wash

How to wash — a gentle rinse

01
The first water, fast
Add cold water, swirl three or four times, and drain at once. The grain drinks fastest on first contact — don't let it sit in dusty water.
02
Gentle turns, no force
Cup your hand and turn the rice with light pressure — the togi motion — ten to fifteen rotations. You are rinsing, not grinding.
03
Repeat until translucent
Fresh water, turn, drain — two or three rounds — until the water is cloudy-translucent, not perfectly clear. Over-washing strips flavour.
04
Drain, then rest
Drain well and let the damp grain stand before cooking, so water reaches the core. Adjust by season — about 30 minutes in summer, but up to an hour in winter, when cold water soaks in more slowly.
Discipline: the whole rinse takes under two minutes. Speed protects the grain — lingering in cloudy water dulls it.

Summer: pull water back 2–3% with a shorter soak. Winter: add 2–3% and soak longer in cold water.

Weigh rice and water on a scale. Volume cups drift 15% or more — precision here separates batches.

The Method · Water

Water quality — use soft water

Cooked rice is more than half water by weight. The water is not just a medium the rice cooks in — it is an ingredient, and its mineral content changes the grain.

Soft water — Japan
Low in minerals軟水
With little dissolved mineral content, starch swells freely and the grain hydrates evenly to its core — tender, glossy, and full of its natural sweetness. Every Japanese variety was bred and judged on soft water.
Hard water — UK & Europe
High in minerals硬水
Hard water carries dissolved calcium and magnesium — the limescale that furs a kettle. Those minerals bind to the grain and slow even absorption, so rice cooks firmer and drier, its sweetness and aroma muted. Chlorine adds an off-note on top.
In Practice
Use filtered or bottled soft water — aim for under 60 mg/L hardness, and soak in it too, not just the pot. The grain drinks most during the rest.

The Method · Cook

How to cook — the professional standard

01
Weigh, wash, rest
Weigh the rice, rinse it gently, drain, and let it stand 30 minutes. Then weigh the water to your chosen ratio — never by eye.
02
Bring up the heat curve
Lid on. Medium-high to a boil over about five minutes, then drop to low and simmer 12–13 minutes. Do not lift the lid.
03
Off the heat — steam
Kill the heat and let it stand, still covered, a full 10 minutes. The residual steam finishes the core and sets the texture.
04
Fluff and release
Now open. Fold with a cut-and-turn motion — shari-kiri — to let steam escape and keep every grain separate and glossy.
Old rule: はじめチョロチョロ、中パッパ — start low, then a strong middle boil. The curve, not the clock alone.

The Method · Mastery

Discipline & common failures

Discipline
Honour the rest
The 10-minute lid-on steam is cooking, not waiting. Plate too early and the centre stays watery.
Lid never lifts mid-cook
Each peek dumps steam and heat, and the rice cooks unevenly. Trust the curve and the timer.
Respect the heat curve
A hard boil to start, a gentle simmer to finish. The rhythm matters more than raw power.
Fluff immediately
Cut and turn the moment the rest ends. Left packed, the bottom compresses and steams to mush.
Common Failures
Gummy & pasty
Cause: surface starch left on, or rice crushed while washing. Fix: rinse gently to translucent, no grinding.
Hard, chalky core
Cause: under-hydrated — no soak, or too little water. Fix: rest 30 min; nudge ratio up.
Scorched base
Cause: heat too high, too long on the simmer. Fix: lower the flame; trust residual steam.
Wet & collapsing
Cause: too much water, or new crop on an old ratio. Fix: pull water back 5%.

もち米
Mochigome — sweet, glutinous rice

A different grain entirely: short, opaque, and near-100% amylopectin, so it turns intensely sticky and chewy. It is soaked and steamed, not boiled — and takes far less added water. Treat it as its own ingredient, not a substitute for table rice.

Bonus · Culture

なぜ日本人は
米を大事にするのか

Why rice is treated as something close to sacred

Economy
For centuries rice (石高) was money, tax, and rank itself. A nation literally counted its wealth in grain.
The Sacred
Offered at shrines, brewed into sake, stacked as kagami-mochi at New Year. Rice is the link between people and the divine.
Language
Gohan means both "cooked rice" and "a meal." The grain is built into the very word for eating.
Not One Grain
Terraced paddies shape the landscape, and a quiet ethic says no grain should be wasted — each one is a year's labour.
